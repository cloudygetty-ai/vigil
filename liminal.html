HOW TO USE THIS APP ▾
1
CHOOSE A SUSPECT SURFACE
Hidden doors are most common in: library/study walls lined with bookcases, paneled hallways with decorative wainscoting, walls adjacent to staircases (triangular dead space behind), basement walls with full-height trim, and any wall where the room math doesn't add up to the exterior footprint.
2
SET UP YOUR SHOT
Stand 3–6 feet from the surface. Fill the entire frame — floor to ceiling if possible. Even lighting is critical: avoid harsh backlighting. Use the TORCH button for dark areas. Switch NIGHT MODE on if the room is dim. Use FREEZE to lock the frame on a detail, then scan from the frozen image.
3
ZOOM IN ON SUSPICIOUS AREAS
Use the zoom slider or pinch gesture to go to 2–5× on trim edges, baseboard corners, and vertical seams. The seam of a hidden door is typically 1–3mm wide — only visible at close range or with raking light. MACRO mode (5×) is best for examining hardware tells.
4
SCAN AND READ THE MARKS
Tap SCAN THIS SURFACE. The app captures the frame and runs Sobel edge detection, symmetry analysis, texture sampling, and shadow gradient scoring. Results show a probability score, zone analysis (floor, ceiling, trim, center, corners), and annotated marks on your image showing exactly where each anomaly was detected.
5
USE THE SENSORS
MAGNETOMETER: tap Start Scan, hold phone steady for baseline, then slowly sweep horizontally along the wall at waist height. A reading spike above 18 µT indicates ferrous metal — door catch, strike plate, or hinge. ACOUSTIC: tap Start Mic, knock firmly with your knuckle on the surface in a grid pattern. Hollow resonance produces a low-frequency dominant FFT signature and triggers the HOLLOW badge.
6
INTERPRET THE RESULT
The MARKS view shows annotated hotspots on your capture — darker ring = higher confidence. The DOOR LOCATION section below your results tells you the predicted door type, its exact position on the wall, and the most likely opening mechanism. The EDGES view shows the raw Sobel output — a hidden door seam appears as a strong continuous vertical line not corresponding to trim or framing.
7
CORROBORATE WITH MANUAL CHECKLIST
Camera analysis alone is not definitive. Use the Manual Inspection Checklist to add physical evidence: hollow knock test, measurement discrepancy, magnet response, light bleed, draught test. Each checked item increases the Suspicion Index. A camera score above 60% combined with 3+ manual checks is strong corroboration.
VISUAL FIELD GUIDE
What to look for: A continuous vertical line running from baseboard to crown molding that doesn't correspond to any known structural element. The gap is typically 1–3mm — barely visible in flat lighting but clearly revealed by raking a flashlight across the surface at a sharp angle. The panel on either side of the seam may have a subtly different paint sheen (one was repainted, the other wasn't). The door seam will run at exactly plumb — a slight lean indicates it's not architectural.
Three hardware signatures: (1) Hinge shadow — at the pivot edge, look for two small parallel rectangles or screw shadows at 32–36 inches and 60–66 inches from floor, slightly recessed into the wall face. (2) Flush pull — a recessed circle or oval in the panel face at handle height (34–40 inches). May be disguised as a decorative boss or knot in wood grain. Run your fingernail across the surface — a flush pull has a distinct lip. (3) Strike plate / magnetic catch — at the latch edge, often near the baseboard or at waist height. A rare-earth magnet will drag noticeably when passed over this zone.
Shadow tells: A concealed door edge creates shadows inconsistent with the room's light source. Stand with the light source behind you — a hidden door seam will cast a shadow slightly deeper than the surrounding wall because the gap behind it absorbs light. Raking light test: hold your phone flashlight almost parallel to the wall surface (grazing angle). Any seam, patch, or raised edge casts a visible shadow invisible under direct light. Floor bleed: in a dark room, shine light on the adjacent room side — door clearance gaps let a sliver of light under the baseboard.
Floor tells — top-down view: A swinging hidden door leaves permanent evidence on the floor. Arc scuff marks — on hardwood, the door edge creates a faint curved scratch pattern. On carpet, a subtle compression line. On tile, a slight glaze wear following the arc. Pivot corner — the hinge pivot point is always at the corner of the arc, against the baseboard. Measure from the arc's center of curvature to find the pivot. Baseboard gap — the baseboard terminates at the door edges. Look for a section of baseboard with slightly different wear, newer paint, or a hairline gap where it meets the door frame.
Bookcase door tells: (1) Off-center alignment — the bookcase appears slightly asymmetric relative to the wall or window it flanks. One side has marginally less clearance or trim. (2) Lever book — one book spine is a false cover that pulls outward rather than tilting. It may feel slightly different from surrounding books — stiffer, or connected by a cable. Check for a book that appears well-read but has no title, or is unusually prominent. (3) Pivot seam — the entire bookcase rotates, so the seam is at its edge against the wall — look for a uniform gap on one side that runs floor to ceiling. (4) No dust on the top of the moving portion — books and shelves that move regularly cannot accumulate the same dust layer as static walls.

DETECTION SIGNATURES
  • ⊡
    SYMMETRY BREAK
    Left/right edge density mismatch, panel width discrepancy, seam at non-architectural boundary
  • ◑
    SHADOW ANOMALY
    Light gradient inconsistent with source, shadow at wrong angle, bleed at floor junction
  • ▦
    TEXTURE MISMATCH
    Paint finish transition, grain direction rotation, drywall compound variance
  • ⬚
    DEAD SPACE
    Interior smaller than exterior footprint, unaccounted volume behind surface
  • ⊕
    HARDWARE TELL
    Flush pull shadow, hinge silhouette, recessed fastener pattern
  • ◎
    WEAR PATTERN
    Arc scuffing on floor, differential paint wear at edge height, baseboard dust line
